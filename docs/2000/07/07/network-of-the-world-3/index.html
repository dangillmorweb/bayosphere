Network of the World

Posted on July 7, 2000 by Dan Gillmor

On Wednesday I mentioned my visit to the London studios of Network of the World, the next step in a plan by Pacific Century Cyberworks to offer a high-speed, satellite-fed Internet access, plus television broadcasts, to tens of millions of Asian households.

Now, more details:

The men in suits follow Michael Johnson into the control room. The front row of workstations, he tells them, were what you’d find in a typical television studio. But the back row of “Web desks,” workstations for producing World Wide Web content, are not.

“You have to think across both platforms,” says Johnson, group creative director for the “Network of the World”, a just-launched broadcast/Internet entertainment service that is an essential piece of a young, Hong Kong-based media baron’s emerging master plan.

The men in suits are bankers, I discover later. They were trying to get a handle on the grandiosely named service, trying to figure out whether it was on track to meet its founder’s grander goal — creating a global communications and media company.

I can’t vouch for the uber-business strategy. But what these folks are creating in this southwest-London studio is at least interesting, maybe even pathbreaking. They’ve latched onto the Internet’s greatest attributes — nearly limitless information plus millions of people who may have something to contribute. They’re adding professional production values and blurring traditional media lines.

It’s all part of a media empire being assembled by Richard Li, the 33-year-old son of Hong Kong’s richest tycoon. In a frenetic series of deals over the past few months, Li’s Pacific Century Group has announced and dealt its way to a sky-high market value. Now it’s aiming for genuine critical mass.

The Network of the World, or Now for short, is the beginnings of the content promised for a pan-Asia, satellite-based broadband Internet and broadcast service that Pacific Century is building. The London studio is Now’s nerve center.

At this point, Now is producing only a couple of hours of original television programming each day. Much more is in the works, including a reported plan to produce lots of online and broadcast educational material.

Like the TV programming — available to satellite owners and viewers whose cable companies carry the signal, mostly in Asia — the closely linked companion Web site (www.now.com) is edgy and mostly entertaining. It’s plainly aimed at a younger crowd, however. And it greatly helps to have a fast Internet connection to view the Web site, which “isn’t your grandma’s Web site,” as Now said briefly on its home page before a real-life grandmother complained via e-mail.

The service is pulling together content across a wide swath of topics. Five are available now, and they emphasize interactivity.

One of the cleverest ideas on Now’s site is the MP3 showcase, where the most popular downloaded music by unknown musicians makes it into a top-10 Now program. Want to get on TV? This is one way.

The “Mother Ship” section of the programming and Web site is about nature in the large sense. Among the features are regular updates on violent weather — viewers can offer Web-camera views of local weather — volcanoes and solar activity. One recent show’s volcano segment discussed the largest eruptions in the solar system, which take place on Io, one of Jupiter’s moons.

The TV presenters aren’t typically the standard-issue, blow-dried types who decorate so many American broadcast-news shows. On-camera talent in the new world of information is about “passion and knowledge,” Johnson says. “It’s not about being pretty.”

Frank Cox, a young research physicist at a British university, is one of the on-camera presenters, and he’s the one explaining Io’s dramatic volcanoes. He’s still learning what to do with his hands when the camera is pointed his way, but his evident knowledge of the topic carries the viewer along.

The Web site is not just an adjunct. It’s integral. Johnson talks about telling stories in linear ways, as on TV, and in the non-linear ways the Web makes possible — and doing so simultaneously.

Will a large audience emerge? I suspect it will. Is there a realistic business model? Beats me.

But plenty about the Now operation is smart. In particular, the producers of this show/site have latched onto the power of participation. They’re tapping the vast knowledge and creativity of the user base, then repackaging it for the wider audience. Cynical exploitation? I don’t think so. People want the larger community to know what they’ve created, or what they know. A potentially worldwide outlet could be quite a lure.

Worldwide is Li’s aim. His most recent dealmaking suggests he might pull it off.

Li and his team persuaded the shareholders of Hong Kong’s flagship telephone company, Cable & Wireless HKT, to sell the company to Pacific Century Cyberworks, a publicly traded company that Li controls. Suddenly, Li will have enormous cash flow to go with his market capitalization, a potent combination as long as he can meet his considerable obligations, one of which is pouring massive amounts of money into Now.

So far, the content is available to anyone who wants it. But Pacific Century expects to sell advertising and launch a variety of related e-commerce services when more viewers tune in, whether on TV or the Web. The Internet analyst community seems more befuddled than enlightened as to whether the plan will pay off.

It will be fascinating to see whether the talent and potential match the ambition here. Richard Li and his team seem almost brazen in their aims. But I’m not going to bet against them.

Spying Questions

The infamous “Echelon” spying network operated by the U.S. and some of our allies has drawn increasing queries from Europe, and now the European Parliament also wants some answers (The Register).

In the United Kingdom, meanwhile, business leaders and civil libertarians are up in arms about a government proposal that is among the most big-brotherish ideas ever concocted. The British government wants pervasive surveillance rights over e-mail and other Internet traffic, as well as the right to demand people’s and companies’ encryption keys in certain circumstances.

The plan is an unworkable mess, but it’s going to cause huge damage if it passes. It may turn out that the British economy takes the worst hit, with businesses moving offshore to avoid the problems this proposal would surely cause. But the civil liberties aspect is even more foul. People may go to jail for refusing to turn over the keys to their entire lives.

I’ve been speaking to people on several sides of this question, and will have more on this in an upcoming column.
